* The "Alpha" of Optimization

** The Pre-History: Joseph Fourier (1826)
*** The Concept of Projection
Before Simplex, Fourier devised a method for solving systems of linear inequalities:
- **Method**: Fourier–Motzkin Elimination.
- **Goal**: Determining the feasibility of undetermined systems.
- **Insight**: Optimization (min-max) is equivalent to solving inequalities.

*** Comparing "Walking" vs. "Projecting"
- **Fourier (1826)**: Project the $n$ -dimensional feasible region onto lower dimensions.
  - *Problem*: Inequality count grows doubly exponentially ($2^{2^n}$).
- **Dantzig (1947)**: Move between vertices of the $n$ -dimensional region.
  - *Advantage*: Usually requires only $m$ to $3m$ iterations (where $m$ is the number of constraints).

*** Historical Note
# Dantzig was unaware of Fourier's work in 1947. He later noted that had he known, he might have been discouraged by the perceived complexity of inequality systems.

Dantzig noted that had he known FME he might have been discouraged by the perceived complexity of inequality systems.

** Why Fourier's Method "Explodes"
- **Simplex**: Complexity depends on the number of *vertices* (usually efficient).
- **Fourier-Motzkin**: Each variable elimination creates a new set of inequalities.
  - If you have $m$ inequalities, eliminating one variable can result in up to $(m/2)^2$ new ones.
  - **The Omega of manual methods**: Impossible for Stigler's 77 variables, but theoretically vital for proving the **Farkas Lemma**.
    

** The "Alpha" of Optimization: The Stigler Diet (1947)
*** The Problem
    George Stigler (1945) sought the minimum cost to feed a 154lb man.
    - **Goal**: Minimize daily cost of food.
    - **Scale**: 77 candidate foods, 9 nutritional constraints.

*** The Mathematical Formulation
    - Minimize $Z = \sum_{j=1}^{77} c_j x_j$

    - Subject to: $\sum_{j=1}^{77} a_{ij} x_j \ge b_i \quad \forall i \in \{1, \dots, 9\}$, \qquad
      $x_j \ge 0 \quad \forall j \in \{1, \dots, 77\}$

    - Where:
      - $c_j$: Cost of food $j$
      - $a_{ij}$: Amount of nutrient $i$ in food $j$
      - $b_i$: RDA for nutrient $i$ (Calories, Protein, Calcium, Iron, Vit A, B1, B2, Niacin, Vit C)

** The Human "Computer": Jack Laderman's Feat
*** Before Machines
In 1947, the "Simplex Method" was brand new (Dantzig). There was no "software."
- **Team**: 9 human clerks at the National Bureau of Standards.
- **Hardware**: Hand-operated desk calculators
- **Effort**: **120 man-days** of computation.

*** Results
- Stigler’s heuristic guess: $39.93/year (1939 prices).
- Laderman’s Simplex solution: $39.69/year.
- *Fun Fact*: The "optimal" diet consisted mostly of wheat flour, evaporated milk, cabbage, spinach, and navy beans. Stigler called it the "human dog biscuit."

** The First Machine Implementation: SEAC & IBM 701
*** SEAC (1951) - The First Electronic Solver
- **Machine**: Standards Eastern Automatic Computer (NBS).
- **Implementation**: First general-purpose Simplex code for a stored-program computer.
- **Language**: **Machine Code / Binary** (no high-level languages like Fortran existed yet).
- **Scale**: Solved a 48x71 Air Force model in minutes.

*** IBM 701 (1954) - The First "Commercial" Code
- **Developer**: William Orchard-Hays at RAND.
- **Language**: **Assembly** (highly optimized for the 701's architecture).
- **Impact**: Could handle ~100 constraints. This code (and the later RSLP1 for IBM 704) moved LP from research to the oil industry (refining/blending).
- **The "Omega" Seed**: This was the start of **Matrix Generators**, the tedious precursors to the modeling languages we use today.


** Before Modeling Languages: Physical Data Entry
*** The Punched Card Era (1950s)
In the "Alpha" phase, there was no ~model.lp~ or ~model.py~. 
Data was physical.

- **Storage**: IBM 80-column punched cards.
- **Method**: One non-zero coefficient per card.
- **Hardware**: IBM 026 Keypunch & 056 Verifier.

** Before Modeling Languages: Workflow
1. **Mathematical Model**: Written on paper.
2. **Matrix Tabulation**: Coefficients mapped to row/column indices.
3. **Keypunching**: Manual transcription to cards.
4. **Verification**: Mechanical "double-checking" of holes.

*** Technical Bottleneck
- A typo in one card meant the Simplex algorithm would diverge or find a wrong solution.
- There was no "Algebraic" check—only a mechanical one.
- **Transition**: This pain directly to the creation of *Matrix Generators* in the 1960s.

** The Representation Gap: Alpha vs. Machine
*** From Human Thought to Solver Input
Before AMLs (like AMPL or JuMP), there was a "lost in translation" phase. 
To a mathematician, the model is a set of summations; to the computer, it is a flat list of entries.

*** Comparison: The Stigler Diet Problem

** The "Middle Ages": Matrix Generators
*** The "Logic" of 1965
    - Instead of writing math, you wrote code to print strings and formatted numbers.
*** Why this was fragile:
    - **Formatting**: The `130 FORMAT` statement strictly controls the 80-column spacing. One mistake here makes the MPS file unreadable.
    - **Maintenance**: To add a nutrient, you must change the `DATA` statements, the `DO` loop limits, and potentially the `FORMAT` strings.

** Anatomy of a Nightmare
- **Fixed Width**: If "WHEAT" was shifted one space to the right, the parser would fail.
- **Flat Structure**: The summation $\sum a_{ij}x_j$ is gone; every $(i, j)$ pair is an individual line.
  - This is exactly the format that was once physically punched into cards.
  - Legacy: Gurobi, CPLEX, ... still read this today for benchmarking.

  
** The "Technical Debt" of the 1970s
- **Decoupling**: In the algebraic form, data ($a_{ij}$) is separate from logic ($\sum$).
- **The MPS Nightmare**: In MPS, data and logic are "fused". 
- If you add one nutrient to the diet, you must recalculate and re-punch the entire file.
- **The Result**: Modeling was slow, error-prone, and required "Model Librarians".
