* The "Alpha" of Optimization

** Background
   - We have a system of linear inequalities.
   - How can we find a feasible solution?
   - In particular, can we choose a feasible solution that optimizes some (linear) criterion?
     - /note: that amounts to optimizing an expression, which can be assigned to a variable./

       
** The Pre-History: Joseph Fourier (1826)
*** The Concept of Projection
    Before the Simplex Method: Fourier devised a method for solving systems of linear inequalities:
    - **Method**: Fourier–Motzkin Elimination.
    - **Goal**: Determining the feasibility of undetermined systems.
    - **Insight**: Optimization (min-max) is equivalent to solving inequalities.

      
** Comparing "Projecting" vs. "Walking"
   - Fourier (1826):
     - Project the $n\text{-dimensional}$ feasible region onto lower dimensions.
   - Dantzig (1947):
     - /*Simplex Method:*/ Move between vertices of the $n\text{-dimensional}$ region.

** Fourier's Method "Explodes"
   - *Fourier-Motzkin*: Each variable elimination creates a new set of inequalities.
     - If we have $m$ inequalities, eliminating one variable can result in up to $(m/2)^2$ new ones.
     - Impractical for the next example (Stigler's 77 variables).
     - Theoretically important (e.g., for proving the Farkas Lemma).
   - *Simplex*: Complexity depends on the number of *vertices* (usually efficient).

** The Stigler Diet Problem (1947)
*** A Problem in the "Alpha" Era
    George Stigler (1945) sought the minimum cost to feed a 154lb man.
    - **Goal**: Minimize daily cost of food.
    - **Scale**: 77 candidate foods, 9 nutritional constraints.

*** Mathematical Formulation
    - Minimize $Z = \sum_{j=1}^{77} c_j x_j$

    - Subject to: $\sum_{j=1}^{77} a_{ij} x_j \ge b_i \quad \forall i \in \{1, \dots, 9\}$, \qquad
      $x_j \ge 0 \quad \forall j \in \{1, \dots, 77\}$

    - Where:
      - $c_j$: Cost of food $j$
      - $a_{ij}$: Amount of nutrient $i$ in food $j$
      - $b_i$: recommended daily allowance for nutrient $i$ \\
        calories, protein, calcium, iron, vitamin A, B1, B2, niacin, vitamin C

        
** The Human "Computer": Jack Laderman's Achievement
*** Before Machines
    In 1947, the "Simplex Method" was brand new (Dantzig). There was no "software."
    - **Team**: 9 human clerks at the National Bureau of Standards.
    - **Hardware**: Hand-operated desk calculators
    - **Effort**: **120 man-days** of computation.

*** Results
    - Stigler’s heuristic guess: $39.93/year (1939 prices).
    - Laderman’s Simplex solution: $39.69/year.
    - *Fun Fact*: The "optimal" diet consisted mostly of wheat flour, evaporated milk, cabbage, spinach, and navy beans. Stigler called it the "human dog biscuit."

      
** First Machine Implementations: SEAC and IBM 701
*** SEAC (1951) - The First Electronic Solver
    - **Machine**: Standards Eastern Automatic Computer (National Bureau of Standards).
    - **Implementation**: First general-purpose Simplex code for a stored-program computer.
    - **Language**: **Machine Code / Binary** (no high-level languages like Fortran existed yet).
    - **Scale**: Solved a $48 \times 71$ Air Force model in minutes.

*** IBM 701 (1954) - The First "Commercial" Code
    - Developed at RAND Corporation.
    - Language: **Assembly** (highly optimized for the 701's architecture).
    - Could handle ~100 constraints
      - moved LP from research to the oil industry (refining/blending).
    - Beginning of **Matrix Generators**
      - (tedious) precursors to the modeling languages we use today.


** Before Modeling Languages: Physical Data Entry
*** The Punched Card Era (1950s)
    In the "Alpha" phase, there was no ~model.lp~ or ~model.py~. 

    Data was physical.

    - **Storage**: IBM 80-column punched cards.
    - **Method**: One non-zero coefficient per card.
    - **Hardware**: IBM 026 Keypunch and 056 Verifier.

** Before Modeling Languages: Workflow
   1. **Mathematical Model**: written on paper.
   2. **Matrix Tabulation**: coefficients mapped to row/column indices.
   3. **Keypunching**: manual transcription to cards.
   4. **Verification**: mechanical "double-checking" of holes.

*** Technical Bottleneck
    - A typo in one card meant the Simplex algorithm would diverge or find a wrong solution.
    - There was no "algebraic" check, only a mechanical one.
    - **Transition**: creation of *Matrix Generators* in the 1960s.
 

** The "Middle Ages": Matrix Generators
*** The "Logic" of 1965
    - Instead of writing math, we wrote code to print strings and formatted numbers.
      - specialized "printing" programs usually written in FORTRAN.
    - Any mistake in the code makes the MPS file unreadable/wrong.
    - **Maintenance**: To add a nutrient, we must change the `DATA` statements, the `DO` loop limits, and potentially the `FORMAT` strings.

** The MPS Format (The Lingua Franca)
   Standardized by IBM in 1965, it remains the "Assembly Language" of LP.
   - **Rigid Structure**: 80-column fixed-field layout (inherited from punched cards).
   - **Sections**: ~NAME~, ~ROWS~, ~COLUMNS~, ~RHS~, ~BOUNDS~.
   - **Persistence**: Every modern solver (Gurobi, CPLEX, HiGHS) still reads .mps files.
    
*** MPS Paradox
   - Highly efficient for computers to parse.
   - Nearly impossible for humans to debug.
   - This "Readability Gap" is what eventually forced the creation of AMLs.

** The Representation Gap
*** From Human Thought to Solver Input
    - Before AMLs (like AMPL or JuMP), there was a "lost in translation" phase.
      - To a mathematician, the model is a set of summations.
      - To the computer, the model is a flat list of entries.

*** Comparison: The Stigler Diet Problem
#+ATTR_LATEX: :align |l|p{7cm}|l|
|----------------------+------------------------------------------------------------+-------------|
| Format               | Representation of $\sum a_{ij}x_j \ge b_i$                 | Readability |
|----------------------+------------------------------------------------------------+-------------|
| **Matrix Generator** | ~FOR I=1 TO 9: WRITE(6,10) ROW(I), RHS(I)~                 | Zero        |
| **MPS (Machine)**    | ~COL001    NUTRIENT01  0.0034~                             |             |
| **Algebraic**        | $\sum_{j \in J} a_{ij} x_j \ge b_i, \quad \forall i \in I$ | High        |
|----------------------+------------------------------------------------------------+-------------|


** MPS: "Technical Debt" of the 1970s
   - In MPS, data and logic are fused:
     - data ($a_{ij}$) is separate from logic (a sum).
     - if we add one nutrient to the diet problem, we must recalculate and re-punch the entire file.
   - Modeling was slow, error-prone, and required "model librarians".
   - Support for linear programming, with limited extensions added:
     - 1965: original MPS, linear programming.
     - 1969: integer variables \to ~INTORG~ and ~INTEND~ markers within the ~COLUMNS~ section.
     - 1970: limits on the variables \to ~BOUNDS~ section
     - 1976: piecewise linear functions \to Special Ordered Sets:
       - ~SOS1~: Only one variable in the set can be non-zero.
       - ~SOS2~: At most two variables can be non-zero, and they must be adjacent (used for approximating curves).
     - 1980s: $x^2$ in the objective: ~QCMATRIX~ or ~QUADOBJ~
   - but format still rather limited...

     
       
* Model taxonomy

** Model Taxonomy: Choosing the Right Solver
*** The "Linear" Foundation
    - *Linear Programming (LP)*: Continuous variables, linear constraints and objective.
      - Standard, typically uses simplex or interior point methods.
    - *Mixed-Integer Programming (MIP)**: Adds discrete choices ($0/1$).
      - NP-Hard problems, typically solved with Branch-and-Bound.
     
*** Beyond Linearity
    - *Nonlinear Programming (NLP)*: Curved surfaces (e.g., chemical blending, power grids).
      - Local vs. Global optima, typically requires gradient-based solvers.
    - *Constraint Programming (CP)*: Logic constraints (e.g., "If A is before B, then C cannot be at 4pm").
      - Applications: Logic programming, Scheduling, Routing.
    - *Complementarity problems*: "Either $x=0$ or the constraint is tight."
      - Applications: Game theory, equilibrium models.

** The "good" modeling language checklist
   1. Decoupling data from the model logic
      - we can change the data and resolve, without touching the model
   2. Solver independence
   3. Declarative (vs. Imperative)
      - tell what the problem is, not how to find a solution
   4. Readability
   5. Handling:
      - presolve phase
      - indexing
      - sparsity
      - solver input/output


** Solver Independence
   - We write the model in a human-readable form (mathematical program).
   - The AML translates it into the specific binary format required by the solver
     - CPLEX
     - IPOPT
     - Gurobi
     - SCIP
     - ...
   - *Benefit*: We can switch solvers with one command.
     - e.g., ~option solver = scip;~.
    


* Modern usage: the "Omega" of Optimization
** Modern usage
   - Dedicated languages (AMPL, GAMS, ...) vs.
   - Embedded libraries (Pyomo, JuMP, GurobiPy, ...)

** 
*** Dedicated AMLs (AMPL, GAMS)
    - **Nature**: Domain-specific languages (DSL).
    - **Strengths**:
      - Syntax is mostly identical to mathematical notation.
      - High-performance presolving and model generation.
      - Support for a vast range of solvers.
    - **Weakness**:
      - Hard to connect to databases, web APIs, or ML libraries (e.g., scikit-learn).\\
        (If used as a standalone system)
     
*** Embedded Libraries (Pyomo, JuMP, GurobiPy)
    - **Nature**: Optimization modeling /inside/ a general-purpose language.
    - **Strength**: Total integration. The model is just an object in a Python/Julia script.
      - Pull data from a SQL database, solve, and push results to a Dashboard in one script.
    - **Weakness**: "Language Overhead"
      - Python can be slower than a dedicated AML for massive model generation.
      - Limited range of solvers supported.


** Syntax Showdown: AMPL vs. Pyomo
:PROPERTIES:

:END:
*** The Objective Function: $\sum_{j} c_j x_j$

#+begin_export latex
\scriptsize
\begin{columns}
\begin{column}{0.5\textwidth}
\textbf{AMPL (Dedicated)}
\begin{verbatim}
var x {FOODS} >= 0;
minimize Cost: 
    sum {j in FOODS} c[j] * x[j];
\end{verbatim}
\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.5\textwidth}
\textbf{Pyomo (Embedded)}
\begin{verbatim}
model.x = Var(model.FOODS, domain=NonNegativeReals)
def obj_rule(model):
    return sum(c[j] * model.x[j] for j in model.FOODS)
model.obj = Objective(rule=obj_rule)
\end{verbatim}
\end{column}
\end{columns}
#+end_export

*** 
    - **AMPL** is shorter and closer to paper math.
    - **Pyomo** is more "verbose" but allows fully using Python language, debuggers, and libraries.


** Modern Usage
*** The Best of Both Worlds
    The modern landscape is no longer a binary choice between "Dedicated" and "Embedded."
    - **The Hybrid Approach**: Using a DSL (AMPL/GAMS) *inside* a GPL (Python/Julia).
    - **The Interface**: Libraries like ~amplpy~ or ~GAMS Transfer~.
    - **Advantages**: 
      1. **Modeling**: The math remains clean, concise, and separate from the "plumbing."
      2. **Execution**: The AMPL engine handles high-speed model generation (C++ core).
      3. **Integration**: E.g., data comes from Pandas/SQL; results go to Plotly/Dash.
     
*** Competitive Advantage
    - **Readability**: The model looks like a paper (AMPL syntax).
    - **Scalability**: Avoids Python's "Global Interpreter Lock" (GIL) during model build.
    - **Deployment**: Easy to containerize (Docker) while keeping the core logic in a DSL.


** The Unified Syntax: amplpy
:PROPERTIES:
:BEAMER_OPT: fragile
:END:
*** From Data Science to Optimization
    Instead of "translating" math into Python loops, we pass the data into an AMPL environment.

#+begin_export latex
\scriptsize
\begin{verbatim}
from amplpy import AMPL
import pandas as pd

# 1. The "Alpha": Pure Algebraic Model
ampl = AMPL()
ampl.eval("""
    set FOODS;
    param cost{FOODS};
    var x{FOODS} >= 0;
    minimize TotalCost: sum{j in FOODS} cost[j] * x[j];
""")

# 2. The "Modern": Python Data Integration
df = pd.read_sql("SELECT name, price FROM market_data", db_conn)
ampl.set_data(df, "FOODS")

# 3. The "Solver": Solver Independence
ampl.set_option('solver', 'gurobi')
ampl.solve()
\end{verbatim}
#+end_export

*** Observations
    - The **Math** is a string (or .mod file), untouched by Python's syntax rules.
    - The **Data** is a DataFrame, handled by Python's best-in-class tools.

    Choice depends on **Build Speed**, **Model Complexity**, and **Production Ecosystem**.

    - If solving **Non-linear Global Optima** \to **GAMS** and **AMPL** are best for solver support.

  
** The Evolution of the Modeling Interface
*** From Physical Cards to Unified APIs
    The "Omega" is not just about syntax; it is about how the *Model* communicates with the *Data* and the *Solver*.

#+ATTR_LATEX: :align |l|l|p{4cm}|
\scriptsize
|-----------------------+--------------------------------------+-------------------------------------------------|
| Era                   | Interface Method                     | Primary Pain Point                              |
|-----------------------+--------------------------------------+-------------------------------------------------|
| **Middle Ages**       | Punched Cards / MPS                  | Manual typos and physical loss                  |
| **Renaissance**       | Matrix Gen $\to$ MPS                 | Rigid "File-based" I/O; logic buried in Fortran |
| **Modern (early)**    | Modeling languages (GAMS, AMPL, ...) | Integration in the deployed system              |
| **Modern (middle)**   | Pure Python (Pyomo)                  | "Language Overhead" (slower model generation)   |
| **Unified Approach**  | **API-driven DSL (amplpy)**          | Licensing and environment complexity            |
|-----------------------+--------------------------------------+-------------------------------------------------|

*** Why "Unified Approach" Wins
    - The math (~.mod~) is separate from the data (~.csv/SQL/...~).
    - Speed: The DSL (AMPL) generates the model in optimized C++ routines.
    - Python handles the "Pre" and "Post" processing (Visualization, ML).
    - We can stop, modify, and re-solve without writing a single file to disk.


** Advanced Topics: Column Generation
:PROPERTIES:
:BEAMER_OPT: fragile
:END:
*** The "Orchestrator" Pattern
    Column generation requires solving a **Master Problem** (MP) and a **Subproblem** (SP) iteratively.

#+begin_export latex
\scriptsize
\begin{verbatim}
# 1. Initialize Master and Subproblem in AMPL
master = AMPL(); master.read("master.mod")
sub    = AMPL(); sub.read("subproblem.mod")

while True:
    master.solve()
    # Extract duals from Master using Python API
    pi = master.get_con('demand_constraints').dual()
    
    # Pass duals as parameters to Subproblem
    sub.get_parameter('pi').set_values(pi)
    sub.solve()
    
    if sub.get_objective('ReducedCost').value() >= -1e-6:
        break  # Optimality reached
        
    # Generate new column (variable) in Master
    new_column = sub.get_variable('x_new').get_values()
    master.eval(f"add var col_{iter} >= 0;") 
    # Use API to update Master's matrix coefficients
\end{verbatim}
#+end_export

*** Why it's "Advanced"
    - The model structure is **dynamic**: we are adding variables *during* the solve.
    - **Unified Omega**: Python handles the logic; AMPL handles the algebra.


** Advanced Topics: Cutting Planes
*** Bridging Heuristics and Exact Math
    In modern usage, "Cuts" are often generated by analyzing the fractional solution of an LP relaxation.
     
    - **The Workflow**:
      1. Solve the **Relaxed Problem** (ignore integrality).
      2. If the solution is fractional, use a **Separation Algorithm** (in Python).
      3. Identify a "Violated Constraint" (e.g., a Gomory cut or Cover cut).
      4. **Inject** that constraint back into the live AMPL object.
      5. Repeat until the solution is integer or no more cuts are found.

*** Advantage of the API
    - No need to restart the solver from scratch.
    - The solver (Gurobi/CPLEX/...) performs a **warm start**, keeping the previous basis.
    - This capability is only possible via memory-resident APIs (~amplpy~), not file-based MPS.

  

* Real-world deployment

** Real-World Deployment: Beyond the Notebook
*** The Modern Infrastructure
    In the 1950s, the "Interface" was a card reader. Today, it is a **Distributed Cloud**.

    - **Cloud Solvers**: Instead of a local license, the API sends the model to a high-performance cluster (Gurobi Cloud, NEOS, Azure/AWS).
      - *Advantage*: Massive parallelization for MIP or Stochastic models.
    - **Model-as-a-Service (MaaS)**:
      - The model is wrapped in a **REST API** (FastAPI/Flask).
      - The UI (Frontend) sends data $\to$ API solves $\to$ UI displays results.

*** Integration with Data Science Pipelines
    - **The Workflow**:
      1. **Pre-processing**: Pandas/SQL clean the "noisy" real-world data.
      2. **Predictive**: ML models (Scikit-learn/XGBoost) predict parameters (e.g., next week's demand).
      3. **Prescriptive**: The **Unified Omega** (AMPL/Python) solves for the optimal plan based on those predictions.
      4. **Post-processing**: Visualization (Plotly/Dash) for the end-user.

     

** Conclusion: From Alpha to Omega
*** The Journey of Abstraction
    - **The Alpha**: Mathematical truth remains constant (Dantzig's Simplex).
    - **The Middle Ages**: Physical struggle (Punched cards, Matrix Generators).
    - **The Renaissance**: Separation of concerns (GAMS/AMPL, Decoupling).
    - **The Omega**: The Unified API (DSL + GPL).

*** Takeaways for the Modern Modeler
    1. **Choose the right taxonomy**: Don't use a MIP solver for a nonlinear problem.
    2. **Decouple everything**: Keep math (~.mod~) separate from the data (~.csv/...~).
    3. **Automate the iteration**: Use APIs (~amplpy~) for Column Gen or Cutting Planes.
    4. **Deploy with purpose**: A model is only useful if it integrates into the "Decision Pipeline."
